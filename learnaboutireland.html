History

On 1 January 1801, the Kingdom of Ireland and the Kingdom of Great Britain were merged to create a United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland

The act of union removed the Irish parliament and located all matters of governance to Westminister in London England.

The UK seen Ireland as a source of Agricultural produce and so Ireland largely missed the industrial revolution of the 19th century.

The Great Famine (1845 - 1851) saw Irelands population of approximately 8.2 million people reduce by a third.

The Great Famine along with a revival of Irish Nationalism through organisations such as the Gaelic Athletic Association saw a shift towards Irish independence in the late 19th Century beginning under Daniel O Connell progressing with Charles Stewart Parnell but ultimately ending in diplomatic failure. The 20th Century would prove to be a new dawn for Ireland

1914 saw Ireland finally achieve "Home Rule" and achieve limited autonomy from the United Kingdom. World War 1 led to the suspension of Home Rule for Ireland until the cessation of hostilities

Easter 1916 saw a group of Irish revolutionary leaders known as the Irish Republican brotherhood led an armed uprising against the rule of the United Kingdom. The rebels seized control of strategic areas of Dublin (Irelands capital). They were defeated swiftly by the crown forces and the leaders of the uprising were execeuted. Public support for the rising was low. However the execeution of the revolutionary leaders did not go down well with the public. Public support turned in favor of the rebels when news broke that the revolutionary leader James Connolly was shot in his wheelchair

Following the rising a young Cork man by the name of Michael Collins redefined the strategy for achieving Irelands liberty. Collins firmly believed that large scale engagements such as the Easter rising would only lead to further defeats. Collins preferred Guerilla Warfare coupled with counter intelligence

From 1919 Irish Republican Army (IRA), launched a three-year guerrilla war, which ended in a truce in July 1921

Collins led a delegation to London which ultimately agreed to the partition of Ireland in the 26 county Free State and the 6 counties of Northern Ireland

March 2004 Ireland becomes the first country in the world to introduce a workplace smoking ban

May 2015 Ireland becomes the first country in the world to legalise marriage for gay couples through popular vote

May 2018 Ireland recognises the equal right to life of the pregnant woman and the unborn.

March 2020 - present Ireland battles the COVID-19 pandemic and becomes one of the first countries to succesfully implement a contact tracing mobile app

Geography

Ireland is located in the north-west of Europe, between latitudes 51° and 56° N, and longitudes 11° and 5° W. It is separated from Great Britain by the Irish Sea and the North Channel, which has a width of 23 kilometres at its narrowest point. To the west is the northern Atlantic Ocean and to the south is the Celtic Sea, which lies between Ireland and Brittany, in France. Ireland has a total area of 84,421 km2 of which the Republic of Ireland occupies 83 percent.

A ring of coastal mountains surround low plains at the centre of the island. The highest of these is Carrauntoohil (Irish: Corrán Tuathail) in County Kerry, which rises to 1,038 m above sea level. The most arable land lies in the province of Leinster. Western areas can be mountainous and rocky with green panoramic vistas. River Shannon, the island's longest river at 386 km long, rises in County Cavan in the north west and flows through Limerick in the mid west.

The mild climate and frequent rainfall, earns Ireland the name of Emerald Isle

The Irish climate is typically insular and is temperate due to the influence of the prevailing southwestern winds from the Atlantic, avoiding the extremes in temperature of many other areas in the world at similar latitudes.

Rain falls throughout the year in Ireland but is light particularly in the east. The west tends to be wetter on average.

There are 55 mammal species in Ireland, and of them only 26 land mammal species are considered native to Ireland.

Some species, such as, the red fox, hedgehog and badger, are very common, whereas others, like the Irish hare, red deer and pine marten are less so. Aquatic wildlife, such as species of sea turtle, shark, seal, whale, and dolphin, are common off the coast.

About 400 species of birds have been recorded in Ireland. Many of these are migratory, including the barn swallow.



Sport

The Gaelic Atheltic Association is the governing body for Irelands national sports Gaelic Football, Hurling and Handball. Ireland is the home of the fastest field sport in the world Hurling.

Gaelic football is the most popular sport in Ireland in terms of match attendance and community involvement, with about 2,600 clubs on the island. In 2003 it represented 34% of total sports attendances at events in Ireland and abroad, followed by hurling at 23%, soccer at 16% and rugby at 8%. The All-Ireland Football Final is the most watched event in the sporting calendar.

Other sports which also command a high level of participation in Ireland include Running, Boxing, Rugby, Horse Racing and Cricket.

Soccer is the most widely played team game on the island and the most popular in Northern Ireland.

International soccer is divided into the Republic of Ireland team and the Northern Ireland National Football Team.

The Republic qualified for the World Cup finals in 1990 (reaching the quarter-finals), 1994, 2002 and the European Championship in 1988, 2012 and 2016.

Northern Ireland qualified for the World Cup finals in 1958 (reaching the quarter-finals), 1982 and 1986 and the European Championship in 2016.

Ireland fields a single national rugby team and a single association, the Irish Rugby Football Union, governs the sport across the island.

The Irish rugby team have played in every Rugby World Cup, making the quarter-finals in six of them.

There are four professional Irish Rugby teams Munster, Connacht, Leinster and Ulster

All four play in the Pro14 and at least three compete for the Heineken Cup.

Culture

Irish traditional music and dance has seen a surge in popularity and global coverage since the 1960s.

Groups and musicians including Horslips, Van Morrison and Thin Lizzy incorporated elements of Irish traditional music into contemporary rock music and, during the 1970s and 1980s, the distinction between traditional and rock musicians became blurred, with many individuals regularly crossing over between these styles of playing. This trend can be seen more recently in the work of artists like Enya, The Saw Doctors, The Corrs, Sinéad O'Connor, Clannad, The Cranberries and The Pogues among others.

The Irish harp, in particular, was of such significance that it became Ireland's national symbol.

The two official languages of the Republic of Ireland are Irish and English.

Irish, though now only the language of a minority, was the vernacular of the Irish people for thousands of years and was possibly introduced during the Iron Age. It began to be written down after Christianisation in the 5th century and spread to Scotland and the Isle of Man, where it evolved into the Scottish Gaelic and Manx languages respectively.

English in Ireland was first introduced during the Norman invasion. It was spoken by a few peasants and merchants brought over from England, and was largely replaced by Irish before the Tudor conquest of Ireland. It was introduced as the official language with the Tudor and Cromwellian conquests.

Robert Boyle was a 17th-century natural philosopher, chemist, physicist, inventor and early gentleman scientist. He is largely regarded as one of the founders of modern chemistry and is best known for the formulation of Boyle's law.

William Thomson, or Lord Kelvin, is the person whom the absolute temperature unit, the kelvin, is named after.

Sir Joseph Larmor, a physicist and mathematician, made innovations in the understanding of electricity, dynamics, thermodynamics and the electron theory of matter. His most influential work was Aether and Matter, a book on theoretical physics published in 1900.
